The structural case for US large-cap equities

Seven converging forces point the same direction over the next decade. Seven credible counter-arguments deserve honest engagement. What the evidence actually supports, and what it doesn't.

Summary

The central claim of this thesis is simple: multiple independent structural forces — wealth concentration, inelastic market dynamics, passive flow mechanics, superstar firm concentration, and AI-driven productivity — are mutually reinforcing and support continued appreciation of US mega-cap equity assets over the next ten to fifteen years.

The investment expression is a continuous dollar-cost-averaged program of leveraged long-equity exposure, held through cycles, with a planned transition to capital preservation at the end of the accumulation phase. This is fundamentally an accumulation strategy with an indefinite holding period rather than a short-dated capture trade.

The argument is neither novel nor fringe. It synthesizes peer-reviewed economics with macro market commentary and empirical finance. What is novel is the combination: each individual mechanism has been challenged, but the convergence of mechanisms is harder to dismiss.

The thesis is defensible. The thesis is not a prediction. The gap between those two statements is where most investment mistakes live.

The honest conclusion: the structural case is credible on a 10–20 year horizon. The chosen expression is reasonably matched to the thesis timescale. The primary risks are not the thesis being wrong, but execution risks: maintaining contributions through major drawdowns, not abandoning the strategy at interim milestones, and correctly executing the accumulation-to-preservation transition when the time comes.

Part I. The Structural Bull Thesis

Core Claim

Over the coming 10 to 15 years, US large-cap equities — and the Nasdaq-100 in particular — will continue to appreciate at rates meaningfully above GDP growth, driven by structural rather than cyclical forces. The appreciation will be punctuated by significant drawdowns (historically 30–50% in the underlying index, 70–90% in leveraged vehicles) but the long-run trajectory is upward and the mechanism is self-reinforcing.

MECHANISM 01

Wealth concentration creates structural asset demand

The empirical fact of rising wealth concentration in the US is well-documented. Piketty, Saez, and Zucman (2018, Quarterly Journal of Economics) established that the share of US wealth held by the top 1% rose from approximately 22% in 1980 to 32% by the late 2010s; the top 0.1% share rose from roughly 7% to 18%.

The critical mechanism connecting wealth concentration to asset prices comes from Mian, Straub, and Sufi (2021, "The Saving Glut of the Rich"). Their finding: income accruing to the top 1% is saved at dramatically higher rates than income accruing to the bottom 90%, and those savings flow overwhelmingly into financial assets. As wealth concentrates, a larger fraction of national income becomes asset demand.

This creates a self-reinforcing loop. Asset price appreciation disproportionately benefits asset holders. The wealthy save a higher fraction of their gains. Those savings flow back into assets. Asset prices rise further. Piketty's famous inequality r > g formalizes the long-run consequence: when return on capital exceeds economic growth, capital accumulates faster than the economy grows.

MECHANISM 02

Markets are far more inelastic than consensus assumes

Gabaix and Koijen's 2021 paper "The Inelastic Markets Hypothesis" fundamentally challenged the consensus view that capital flows have modest price impact. Their finding: a dollar of flow into equity markets moves aggregate market capitalization by approximately five dollars.

The mechanism is supply-side. The float of investable equity is small relative to capital seeking returns, and most institutional holders are price-inelastic. The implication: any persistent source of flows — 401(k) contributions, passive indexing, demographic savings, foreign capital — produces persistent upward price pressure.

MECHANISM 03

Passive flows create a price-insensitive bid

Chinco and Sammon estimate true passive ownership of US equities at approximately 38%, far above the commonly cited 15% figure. The difference comes from including closet indexers, smart beta strategies, and effectively passive active managers.

Passive flows are entirely price-insensitive. A 401(k) participant contributing biweekly does not check valuations. Federal Reserve Flow of Funds data documents total defined contribution plan assets exceeding $11 trillion, with $500–700 billion in net annual contributions. Combined with Gabaix-Koijen's elasticity estimate, these flows alone can account for a meaningful fraction of observed equity appreciation.

MECHANISM 04

Superstar firms capture disproportionate returns

Autor, Dorn, Katz, Patterson, and Van Reenen (2020, "The Fall of the Labor Share and the Rise of Superstar Firms") document that a small number of highly productive firms are capturing growing shares of industry revenue and profits. The labor share of income has fallen; the capital share has correspondingly risen.

Most superstar firms are in the Nasdaq-100. Bessembinder's 2018 paper provides perhaps the most important observation in modern equity research: essentially all long-run equity premium comes from a tiny minority of stocks. The top 4% of companies account for all net lifetime wealth creation above Treasury bills. NDX's concentration in superstar-firm candidates is not a risk — it is the feature that drives the premium.

MECHANISM 05

Capital share of income is rising

Greenwald, Lettau, and Ludvigson's 2019 paper "How the Wealth Was Won" provides the accounting decomposition that ties the mechanisms together. Analyzing US equity returns from 1989 to 2017, they find that most of the rise in equity values came from redistribution from labor to capital — from rising profit margins — rather than from productivity growth.

Equity values are not appreciating primarily because the economy is growing faster. They are appreciating because a rising share of economic output is being captured by equity owners. If this redistribution continues, equity appreciation continues structurally.

MECHANISM 06

Monetary policy asymmetry (the Fed put)

Cieslak and Vissing-Jorgensen (2021) provide rigorous empirical evidence for what market participants have long asserted: Federal Reserve policy responds asymmetrically to equity market moves. Declines are met with more forceful response than equivalent rallies.

The implication is not that crashes cannot happen, but that they are self-limiting. The policy response to a 20%+ equity drawdown is predictably accommodative, which shortens downturns and accelerates recoveries. Combined with the safe asset shortage documented by Caballero, Farhi, and Gourinchas, structural demand for US equities during stress is paradoxically elevated, not diminished.

MECHANISM 07

AI-driven productivity concentrated in mega-cap tech

The AI productivity argument is the newest and most contested pillar. Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2023) document measurable productivity gains from LLM deployment. Industry reports project 1–2% annual GDP growth contribution over the coming decade.

Critically, AI infrastructure and returns are concentrating in a small number of companies: the hyperscalers, the chip designers, and their dependent ecosystem. All are NDX constituents. If AI produces even half the projected gains, earnings growth will flow disproportionately to NDX.

The Convergent Argument

No single mechanism above is sufficient. Any one could be wrong or overstated without destroying the thesis. The strength is in the convergence: seven independent mechanisms, each empirically documented in peer-reviewed literature, each pointing in the same direction, each operating on a multi-decade rather than cyclical timescale.

For the thesis to be wrong, several of these mechanisms would need to reverse simultaneously. Wealth concentration would need to decrease. Passive flows would need to stop or reverse. Superstar firms would need to lose their competitive advantages. Capital share would need to fall. Any single reversal is possible. The joint probability of multiple reversals within a decade is low.

Part II. Counter-Arguments

Intellectual honesty requires engaging the strongest versions of the opposing case. Each of the following comes from serious academic or empirical sources. Each, individually, would meaningfully weaken the thesis if correct.

COUNTER 01

Most of the capital share rise is housing, not equity

Matthew Rognlie's 2015 Brookings paper presents the most rigorous academic challenge to Piketty's framework. When Piketty's data is decomposed by asset class, the overwhelming majority of the rise in capital's share of income comes from housing, not from corporate equity.

If Rognlie is correct, wealth concentration would continue to drive up housing prices but would provide limited structural support for corporate equity appreciation. The cleanest expression of the wealth concentration thesis would be real estate, not NDX.

Strength Substantial. Rognlie's methodology is accepted by the profession. The thesis-builder must either rebut directly or narrow the claim.
COUNTER 02

The inequality data itself may be overstated

Auten and Splinter's 2024 paper re-examines US income inequality measurements. Once proper adjustments are made for taxes, transfers, and underreported income in lower quintiles, the rise in inequality since 1980 is substantially smaller than Piketty-Saez-Zucman measurements suggest — perhaps 2–3 percentage points rather than 6–8.

Strength Moderate to substantial. Live academic debate; neither side has definitively prevailed. Even adjusted figures show rising concentration.
COUNTER 03

The "this time is different" problem

Every generation of investors has identified structural forces they believed would produce sustained exceptional returns. The Nifty Fifty. Japan in the 1980s. Dot-com. The 2006–2007 housing bubble. In each case, structural arguments were produced by serious economists, documented in peer-reviewed literature, and used to justify elevated valuations. In each case, the structural argument turned out to be wrong or insufficient.

The base rate for "new era" theses being right is low. John Hussman's research documents that historical valuation measures have predicted future 10–12 year returns with remarkable consistency across regimes. Current US equity valuations are near or above levels that have historically preceded decade-long periods of subpar returns.

Strength Significant as a Bayesian prior. The thesis may be right, but the prior on structural theses should be low.
COUNTER 04

AI productivity claims may be wrong

Daron Acemoglu's 2024 paper provides the most prominent academic skepticism of AI productivity claims. His estimate of AI's productivity impact over the next decade is an order of magnitude smaller than industry consensus — closer to 0.05% annual GDP contribution than 1–2%.

If Acemoglu is correct, a major pillar of the NDX-concentration argument collapses. The current AI capex boom would be a misallocation of capital, and the earnings growth priced into mega-cap tech would not materialize.

Strength Moderate. Nobel laureate, serious methodology, but a model-based projection rather than empirical observation.
COUNTER 05

Valuations may already price in the structural case

Research Affiliates, GMO, and other valuation-focused firms have shown US large-cap equities offering low or negative real 10-year expected returns for the past decade. For the past decade, these projections have been wrong.

The argument is not that valuation-based forecasts are always wrong. It is that valuations eventually matter. At current CAPE ratios and market cap to GDP near historical highs, the mathematical relationship between starting valuation and long-run returns suggests most future appreciation may already be priced in.

Strength Moderate. Valuations eventually matter; the timing is notoriously unpredictable.
COUNTER 06

The Fed put may be politically unavailable

The empirical Fed-put evidence comes from roughly 1994–2020 — a period of structurally low and stable inflation. In that environment, the Fed could respond to drawdowns with easing without violating its price stability mandate.

The post-2021 inflation shock and restoration of positive real rates may have structurally changed this. If inflation is structurally higher going forward — from deglobalization, demographic change, geopolitical fragmentation, or fiscal dominance — the Fed may be unable to cut rates aggressively without triggering inflation. The Fed put may be politically available only when inflation is already low.

Strength Significant and underappreciated. This is the most important counter-argument on a 10–15 year horizon. The 1966–1982 period saw US equities return essentially nothing in real terms over 16 years.
COUNTER 07

Concentration itself is a risk factor

The Nasdaq-100 is currently more concentrated in its top holdings than at any point in its history. A strategy justified by the structural case for NDX is implicitly a concentrated bet on a small number of companies.

Bessembinder cuts both ways. If all long-run equity premium comes from a small minority of stocks, betting on the current minority requires those specific companies to continue dominating. The Magnificent 7 of 1999 did not resemble the Magnificent 7 of 2024. There is no reason to assume the Magnificent 7 of 2035 will resemble today's.

Strength Modest to moderate. The thesis doesn't require specific companies to persist if NDX composition changes over time.

Part III. Conclusion & Framework

What the Evidence Supports

The structural bull thesis for US large-cap equities is credible on a 10–15 year horizon. The mechanisms have strong peer-reviewed empirical support. Any one alone is a weak basis for positioning; their convergence is genuinely persuasive.

The counter-arguments are not strawmen. Each represents a serious challenge from credible sources, and in aggregate they establish that the thesis is contested rather than consensus. The Fed-put fragility argument is the most important one on this specific horizon — it describes a scenario (regime change to persistent inflation) that would weaken multiple pillars of the thesis simultaneously.

Matching Expression to Thesis

Investment expressions of this thesis fall into two categories. Understanding which a given expression belongs to is critical, because they face fundamentally different risks.

Case A — Fixed-date capture strategies

Strategies structured to produce a target outcome by a specific date, typically within 3–5 years. These are poorly matched to the structural thesis. The thesis operates on decades; fixed-date strategies operate on cyclical timescales where interim variation (70–90% drawdowns in leveraged vehicles) dominates outcomes. Success in a fixed-date expression measures luck on a specific path, not the validity of the underlying thesis.

Case B — Long-horizon accumulation with planned preservation transition

Strategies structured to accumulate leveraged equity exposure continuously over a multi-year horizon, with no fixed exit date and with a planned eventual transition to capital preservation. This category is reasonably matched to the thesis. The accumulation timescale aligns with when structural forces operate. DCA mechanics turn drawdowns into accumulation opportunities. The planned preservation transition provides the exit pathway fixed-date strategies lack.

Leveraged lifecycle investing in this form has academic support beyond the structural thesis itself. Ayres and Nalebuff's Lifecycle Investing (2010) argues that young investors with long accumulation horizons should take on leveraged equity exposure early to achieve better lifetime risk-adjusted outcomes. The mechanism is temporal diversification.

The Strategic Framework

A 10–15 year leveraged accumulation strategy expressing this thesis requires clarity on three phases. Most long-horizon strategies fail in the transition, not the accumulation.

Phase 01
Accumulation
Years 1 through 10–13
  • Continuous contributions regardless of market conditions
  • Mechanical position sizing, no discretion
  • Pre-committed hold-through-drawdown policy
  • Interim milestones as checkpoints, not exit criteria
  • Annual thesis review
Phase 02
Transition
Final 2–3 years of accumulation
  • Gradual reduction in new leveraged adds
  • Progressive reallocation to preservation assets
  • Tax-aware sequencing of gains
  • Cash buffer accumulation
  • Pre-committed completion trigger
Phase 03
Preservation
Post-transition
  • Bonds, Treasuries, diversified index funds
  • Leverage removed from the portfolio
  • Withdrawal planning under stress
  • Rebalancing discipline to target allocation

Primary Execution Risks

The thesis and framework are only as useful as the execution. The primary risks on this horizon are behavioral, not thesis-based.

The midlife crisis of confidence

Somewhere in years 5–8, during or after a major drawdown, the strategy will face its hardest test. The account will be down significantly, media will be uniformly pessimistic, and continuing to contribute will feel irrational. The thesis does not protect against this; only pre-committed rules do.

The premature success temptation

If the strategy performs well early, there will be strong temptation to stop, take gains, and declare victory. Interim milestone achievement is the most common cause of abandoning long-horizon strategies. The accumulation timescale, not the interim balance, is what matters.

The transition-timing trap

Switching too early gives up years of compounding. Switching too late exposes the portfolio to a sequence-of-returns shock right before preservation. The transition must be pre-planned, phased, and mechanical.

Life-event pressure

Job changes, family health, divorce, aging parents, major purchases — any can pressure the investor to pull capital. The plan must accommodate contribution pauses without liquidation.

Final Assessment

The structural bull thesis is defensible on a 10–15 year horizon. The mechanisms are real, documented, and mutually reinforcing. Serious counter-arguments exist, particularly around policy regime change, but do not rise to definitive refutation.

A leveraged dollar-cost-averaged accumulation strategy with planned preservation transition is a reasonable expression of the thesis. It matches the thesis timescale, uses DCA to convert drawdowns into accumulation opportunities, and provides the exit pathway that fixed-date strategies lack.

The thesis supports the strategy. The strategy does not guarantee the outcome. The difference is not a failure of either — it is the inherent nature of probabilistic long-horizon investing.

An investor who understands this distinction, accepts it, and executes accordingly has given themselves a legitimate chance at the structural case paying off over the accumulation period. An investor who conflates the two — who takes thesis validity as outcome certainty — will make mistakes during the inevitable interim drawdowns that cost far more than the structural thesis can repay.

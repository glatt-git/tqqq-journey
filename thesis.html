Summary

My core claim is pretty simple. Several forces are pushing US mega-cap equity prices higher, they reinforce each other, and I don't see any of them reversing over the next decade or two. Wealth keeps concentrating. Markets are far more inelastic than people assume. Passive flows create a bid that doesn't care about price. A tiny number of superstar firms capture most of the economic gains, and they're almost all in the Nasdaq-100. AI is real, and its returns are concentrating in those same companies. That's the case, and it's why I'm comfortable putting leveraged money on it.

How I'm expressing that view is a continuous DCA program of leveraged long-equity exposure, held through whatever the market does, with a planned handoff to capital preservation when I get close to my target. This isn't a short-dated trade where I'm trying to catch a specific window. It's an accumulation strategy with no fixed exit date.

None of the individual pieces of this argument are mine. They come from peer-reviewed economics, macro commentary, and empirical finance research. What I'm doing is assembling them into a single story. Any one mechanism can be challenged on its own merits. I find the convergence of all of them harder to wave away.

I can defend the thesis. That doesn't mean I'm predicting the outcome. Most investment mistakes I've seen come from collapsing that gap.

Where I land: the case is credible on a 10 to 20 year horizon, and the strategy I've chosen roughly matches that timescale. What worries me most isn't the thesis being wrong. It's execution. Whether I can keep contributing when the market is down 60%, whether I'll resist pulling the plug at an interim milestone that looks big but isn't target, and whether I'll correctly time the shift from accumulation to preservation when that moment comes.

Part I. The Structural Bull Thesis

Core Claim

Over the next 10 to 15 years, I expect US large-cap equities, and the Nasdaq-100 especially, to keep appreciating at rates well above GDP growth. The forces driving this look structural to me, not cyclical. I fully expect the ride to include 30 to 50% drawdowns in the index and 70 to 90% drawdowns in leveraged vehicles like the one I'm using. The long-run trajectory is still up, and each downturn tends to feed the next leg higher because the mechanism keeps reinforcing itself.

MECHANISM 01

Wealth concentration creates structural asset demand

Wealth concentration in the US is well-documented. Piketty, Saez, and Zucman showed that the top 1% share of US wealth went from about 22% in 1980 to 32% by the late 2010s, and the top 0.1% went from around 7% to 18% over the same period.

The piece that matters for asset prices comes from Mian, Straub, and Sufi. They showed that income flowing to the top 1% gets saved at dramatically higher rates than income flowing to the bottom 90%, and those savings go overwhelmingly into financial assets. So as wealth concentrates, a bigger share of national income becomes asset demand.

What I find compelling here is the feedback loop. Asset prices rise. The people who benefit most are the same people who save the highest fraction of their gains. Their savings flow back into financial assets. Prices rise further. Piketty's r > g inequality captures the long-run version: when the return on capital beats economic growth, capital accumulates faster than the economy grows.

MECHANISM 02

Markets are far more inelastic than consensus assumes

Gabaix and Koijen's paper on the inelastic markets hypothesis changed how I think about flow effects. The old consensus was that capital flows had modest price impact. What they showed is that a dollar of net flow into equity markets moves aggregate market cap by roughly five dollars.

The reason comes down to supply. There isn't that much freely-floating investable equity relative to the capital chasing returns, and most institutional holders are price-inelastic in the short run. Any persistent source of flow keeps pushing prices up. 401(k) contributions, passive indexing, demographic savings, foreign capital looking for safe stores of value. None of that goes away soon.

MECHANISM 03

Passive flows create a price-insensitive bid

Chinco and Sammon put true passive ownership of US equities at about 38%, which is way higher than the commonly quoted 15%. They get there by counting closet indexers, smart beta strategies, and active managers whose behavior is effectively passive.

Passive flows don't care about valuation. Your average 401(k) participant contributing biweekly isn't checking the P/E ratio before each paycheck. Total defined contribution plan assets are over $11 trillion according to Fed Flow of Funds data, with $500 to $700 billion in net annual contributions. Plug those flow numbers into the Gabaix-Koijen elasticity and you can account for a chunky fraction of observed equity appreciation from this channel alone.

MECHANISM 04

Superstar firms capture disproportionate returns

Autor, Dorn, Katz, Patterson, and Van Reenen documented that a small number of highly productive firms are capturing larger and larger shares of industry revenue and profits. Labor's share of income has fallen, and capital's share has risen to match.

Most of these superstar firms sit in the Nasdaq-100. Bessembinder's research reshaped how I think about equity premium. Almost all the long-run equity premium comes from a tiny minority of stocks. The top 4% of companies account for all the net lifetime wealth creation above T-bills. NDX's concentration in superstar-firm candidates is the feature. It's where the premium lives.

MECHANISM 05

Capital share of income is rising

Greenwald, Lettau, and Ludvigson did the accounting work that ties all this together. Looking at US equity returns from 1989 to 2017, they found that most of the rise in equity values came from redistribution away from labor toward capital via rising profit margins. Productivity growth wasn't the main driver.

That reframes what's happening with equity prices. They're not up primarily because the economy is growing faster. They're up because equity owners are capturing a larger share of whatever economic output exists. As long as that redistribution continues, equity appreciation continues structurally.

MECHANISM 06

Monetary policy asymmetry (the Fed put)

Traders have called this the Fed put for decades. Cieslak and Vissing-Jorgensen ran the numbers and confirmed it. Fed policy responds asymmetrically to equity market moves, with declines getting a more forceful response than equivalent rallies.

This doesn't mean crashes can't happen. It means they tend to be self-limiting. When you see a 20%+ drawdown, the policy response is predictably accommodative, which shortens the downturn and speeds up the recovery. Layer in Caballero, Farhi, and Gourinchas's work on the global safe asset shortage, and you get a weird result: structural demand for US equities during stress episodes is actually higher than in calm periods.

MECHANISM 07

AI-driven productivity concentrated in mega-cap tech

This is the newest and most contested pillar of my thesis. Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond have documented measurable productivity gains from LLM deployment in real workflows. Industry forecasts put AI's contribution to annual GDP growth somewhere in the 1 to 2% range over the coming decade.

What matters for my positioning is that AI infrastructure and returns are concentrating in a handful of companies. The hyperscalers building the datacenters, the chip designers making the silicon, the ecosystem depending on both. They're all NDX constituents. Even if AI produces half of what industry expects, the earnings growth flows disproportionately into the index I'm betting on.

The Convergent Argument

None of these seven arguments on its own would make me comfortable taking this position. Any one of them could be wrong or overstated, and the thesis would survive. What I'm betting on is that they all point in the same direction and operate on multi-decade timescales rather than cyclical ones.

For the full case to be wrong, several of these would have to reverse at roughly the same time. Wealth concentration would need to decrease, passive flows would need to stop or reverse, superstar firms would need to lose their competitive advantages, and capital's share of income would need to fall. I can imagine any single one of those happening. I struggle to imagine three or four of them happening simultaneously within a decade.

Part II. Counter-Arguments

I didn't want to build this thesis without stress-testing it against the best opposing arguments I could find. What follows are the seven counter-arguments that are most compelling. Each comes from a serious source. Each on its own would take a real bite out of the case if it turned out to be correct.

COUNTER 01

Most of the capital share rise is housing, not equity

Matthew Rognlie's Brookings paper is the most rigorous academic challenge to Piketty that I've found. When he decomposes Piketty's capital-share data by asset class, almost all of the increase comes from housing, not from corporate equity.

If Rognlie is right, wealth concentration would still drive housing prices higher but would offer limited structural support for corporate equity appreciation. The cleanest way to express the wealth concentration thesis would be buying real estate, not NDX.

Strength Substantial. His methodology is accepted by the profession. The caveat here is that more and more people cannot afford to own homes, so I chose the most accessible vehicle - the stock market.
COUNTER 02

The inequality data itself may be overstated

Auten and Splinter re-examined US income inequality measurements. Once you adjust for taxes, transfers, and underreported income in lower quintiles, the rise in inequality since 1980 is much smaller than the Piketty-Saez-Zucman numbers suggest. Something closer to 2 or 3 percentage points rather than 6 to 8.

Strength Moderate to substantial. This is a live academic debate where neither side has definitively won. That said, even after the downward revisions, concentration is still rising.
COUNTER 03

"This time is different" is what everyone says

Every generation thinks they've spotted the thing. The Nifty Fifty in the '70s. Japan in the '80s. Dot-com. Housing in 2006. Each time, serious economists wrote serious papers explaining why the elevated valuations made sense. Each time, they were wrong, or at least not right enough to justify what people paid.

That's uncomfortable for me. I'm making a structural argument too. And John Hussman has spent fifteen years showing that when you actually measure valuations, they predict 10- to 12-year returns pretty reliably. By those measures, we're at levels that historically precede lost decades.

Strength Real. I can believe my thesis and still admit the base rate for theses like mine is low. Both are backed by data.
COUNTER 04

AI productivity claims may be wrong

Daron Acemoglu's paper is the most prominent academic skepticism of AI productivity claims I've read. He estimates AI's decade productivity impact an order of magnitude smaller than industry consensus. Closer to 0.05% annual GDP contribution than 1 to 2%.

If he's right, one of the pillars of my NDX concentration argument collapses. The current AI capex boom would be a misallocation of capital, and the earnings growth priced into mega-cap tech wouldn't materialize.

Strength Moderate. He's a Nobel laureate with serious methodology, though his estimate is a model-based projection rather than direct empirical observation of what's already happening.
COUNTER 05

Valuations may already price in the structural case

Research Affiliates, GMO, and other valuation-focused firms have been forecasting low or negative real 10-year returns for US large-cap equities for the past decade. For the past decade, those projections have been wrong.

The stronger version of this argument isn't that valuation models are always wrong. It's that valuations eventually matter, even when they don't for long stretches. With CAPE ratios and market cap to GDP near historical highs, the mathematical relationship between starting valuation and long-run returns suggests a lot of future appreciation may already be priced in.

Strength Moderate. Valuations do eventually matter. The timing of "eventually" is notoriously unpredictable.
COUNTER 06

The Fed put may be politically unavailable

The empirical Fed put evidence comes from roughly 1994 through 2020, a 26-year window of structurally low and stable inflation. In that environment, the Fed could respond to drawdowns with easing without violating its price stability mandate.

The post-2021 inflation shock and the restoration of positive real rates may have structurally changed this. If inflation stays higher going forward, whether from deglobalization, demographic change, geopolitical fragmentation, or fiscal dominance, the Fed may not be able to cut rates aggressively without triggering more inflation. The Fed put may be politically available only when inflation is already low.

Strength Significant, and I think underappreciated. This is the most important counter-argument on my 10 to 15 year horizon. From 1966 through 1982, US equities returned essentially nothing in real terms over 16 years. That's the scenario I have to hold against my thesis.
COUNTER 07

Concentration itself is a risk factor

The Nasdaq-100 is currently more concentrated in its top holdings than at any point in its history. Any strategy justified by the structural case for NDX is implicitly a concentrated bet on a small number of companies.

Bessembinder's work cuts both ways here. If almost all long-run equity premium comes from a tiny minority of stocks, betting on the current minority requires those specific companies to stay on top. The Magnificent 7 of 1999 looked nothing like the Magnificent 7 of 2024. I have no reason to assume the Magnificent 7 of 2035 will look like today's.

Strength Modest to moderate. The thesis doesn't strictly require specific companies to persist as long as NDX composition evolves with whoever the new leaders are.

Part III. Conclusion & Framework

What the Evidence Supports

After working through both sides, I firmly believe the structural bull thesis for US large-cap equities is credible on a 10 to 15 year horizon. The mechanisms have strong peer-reviewed empirical support. No single one of them is enough to justify this position on its own. Taken together though, they're persuasive to me.

The counter-arguments aren't strawmen. Each is a real challenge from a credible source, and collectively they establish that the thesis is contested rather than consensus. The Fed-put fragility argument is the one that matters most on my specific horizon. It describes a scenario, regime change to persistent inflation, that would weaken multiple pillars of my thesis at the same time.

Matching Expression to Thesis

There are really two ways to express this kind of thesis in actual positioning, and they face very different risks. I want to be clear about which category my own strategy belongs to.

Case A. Fixed-date capture strategies

Strategies built to hit a target outcome by a specific date, typically within 3 to 5 years. I think these are poorly matched to a structural thesis. The thesis operates on decades. Fixed-date strategies operate on cyclical timescales, where interim variation dominates outcomes. When you're running leveraged exposure through a 70 to 90% drawdown on a 3-year clock, you're measuring luck on one specific path. You're not actually testing the underlying structural case.

Case B. Long-horizon accumulation with planned preservation transition

Strategies built to accumulate leveraged equity exposure continuously over a multi-year horizon, with no fixed exit date and a planned eventual shift to capital preservation. This is the category my strategy falls into, and I think it's reasonably matched to the thesis. The accumulation timescale aligns with when the structural forces actually operate. DCA mechanics convert drawdowns into accumulation opportunities instead of catastrophes. The planned preservation shift gives me an exit pathway that fixed-date strategies simply don't have.

Leveraged lifecycle investing in this form also has academic support beyond my thesis itself. Ayres and Nalebuff argued that young investors with long accumulation horizons should take on leveraged equity exposure early to get better lifetime risk-adjusted outcomes. The mechanism they describe is temporal diversification.

The Strategic Framework

A 10 to 15 year leveraged accumulation strategy needs clarity on three distinct phases. From what I've read about long-horizon strategies that failed, the biggest risks show up in the transition phase, not the accumulation phase. Knowing that ahead of time is the point of writing this down.

Phase 01
Accumulation
Years 1 through 10–13
  • Continuous contributions regardless of market conditions
  • Mechanical position sizing, no discretion
  • Pre-committed policy for holding through drawdowns
  • Interim milestones as checkpoints, not exit criteria
  • Annual thesis review
Phase 02
Transition
Final 2–3 years of accumulation
  • Gradual reduction in new leveraged adds
  • Progressive reallocation to preservation assets
  • Tax-aware sequencing of gains
  • Cash buffer accumulation
  • Pre-committed completion trigger
Phase 03
Preservation
Post-transition
  • Bonds, Treasuries, diversified index funds
  • Leverage removed from the portfolio
  • Withdrawal planning under stress
  • Rebalancing discipline to target allocation

Primary Execution Risks

Thesis and framework are only as useful as the execution. From what I've read about long-horizon strategies that failed, the biggest risks on this horizon are behavioral, not thesis-based.

The midlife crisis of confidence

Somewhere in years 5 to 8, likely during or right after a major drawdown, I'm going to face the hardest test of this whole thing. The account will be down a lot, media will be uniformly pessimistic, and continuing to contribute will feel irrational. My thesis won't protect me from this moment. Only pre-committed rules will.

The premature success temptation

If the strategy performs well early, I'm going to feel strong temptation to stop, take the gains, and declare victory. Interim milestone achievement is supposedly the most common cause of abandoning long-horizon strategies. What matters is the accumulation timescale I committed to, not whatever the account shows at some arbitrary point along the way.

The transition-timing trap

Switching to preservation too early gives up years of compounding that were supposed to be the whole point. Switching too late exposes the portfolio to a sequence-of-returns shock right before I need the money. The transition has to be pre-planned, phased, and mechanical. I don't trust my in-the-moment judgment for something this important.

Life-event pressure

Job changes, family health issues, divorce, aging parents, major purchases. Any of these can create pressure to pull capital out. My plan has to accommodate contribution pauses without forcing liquidation.

Final Assessment

So here's where I end up. The structural bull thesis is defensible on a 10 to 15 year horizon. The mechanisms are real, documented, and reinforce each other. Serious counter-arguments exist, especially around policy regime change, though none of them rise to the level of a knockout.

A leveraged DCA accumulation strategy with planned preservation transition is a reasonable way to express that view. It matches the thesis timescale, it converts drawdowns into buying opportunities via DCA instead of exit triggers, and it provides the graceful exit pathway that fixed-date strategies don't have.

The thesis supports the strategy. The strategy doesn't guarantee the outcome. That gap isn't a failure of either one. It's just what probabilistic long-horizon investing is.

If I can hold that distinction clearly in my head and execute accordingly, I've given myself a legitimate chance at the structural case paying off over my accumulation period. If I collapse the two and treat thesis validity as outcome certainty, I'll make mistakes during the inevitable drawdowns that cost far more than any structural thesis can repay.
